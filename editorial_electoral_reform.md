# Electoral Reform in India: The Unfinished Agenda of Democratic Renewal

---

**Relevant for:** GS Paper II (Polity & Governance) — Representation of People Act, Electoral Process, Constitutional Bodies, Federalism

---

## Introduction

India's democratic architecture — the largest electoral exercise in human history — rests on a paradox. The nation that conducts elections with near-clockwork precision for over 960 million eligible voters has simultaneously allowed the structural foundations of that process to erode through opaque funding, criminalisation of politics, and an increasingly contested regulatory framework. The Supreme Court's landmark February 2024 verdict striking down the Electoral Bond Scheme as unconstitutional has reopened the question that five decades of committee reports have asked but never resolved: how should India fund, regulate, and conduct its elections in a manner worthy of its constitutional promise?

This editorial examines the five pillars of electoral reform — political funding, Election Commission independence, simultaneous elections, delimitation, and digital disruption — and argues that India stands at an inflection point where piecemeal reform is no longer sufficient.

---

## I. Political Funding: From Electoral Bonds to the Next Framework

The Electoral Bond Scheme (2018–2024) was, by scale, the most significant experiment in political funding India had ever attempted. Over its lifespan, bonds worth Rs 16,518 crore were purchased across 30 tranches, constituting nearly 56 per cent of all political funding in the country. The Supreme Court's unanimous five-judge bench ruling held the scheme violative of Article 19(1)(a) — the citizen's fundamental right to information — and found that its opacity fostered conditions conducive to quid pro quo arrangements between corporations and political parties.

The verdict, however, created a vacuum. India has returned to the pre-2018 status quo, where cash donations below Rs 20,000 remain anonymous and unaccounted for — the very problem the bonds were ostensibly designed to solve.

### What the Committees Recommended

The trajectory of reform thinking on this question is instructive:

- **The Indrajit Gupta Committee (1998)** advocated state funding of elections — but strictly in kind, not cash. It proposed a Rs 600 crore annual corpus from the Centre, matched equally by states, to provide recognised parties with printing material, electronic media time, vehicles, and fuel.
- **The Law Commission's 170th Report (1999)** went further, recommending total state funding conditional on parties being barred from raising funds from any other source — but only after a regulatory framework ensuring internal party democracy, audited accounts, and mandatory disclosure was in place.
- **The Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990)** had earlier recommended limiting election expenses, banning government resource misuse, and strengthening the Election Commission's investigative powers.

The common thread across five decades of recommendations — from Tarkunde (1974) to the Law Commission (1999) — is that state funding cannot precede party regulation. Yet India has attempted neither comprehensively.

### The Way Forward

A credible post-Electoral Bond framework must rest on three pillars: (1) mandatory real-time digital disclosure of all donations above Rs 2,000, (2) a National Election Fund managed by the Election Commission for partial state funding in kind, and (3) statutory regulation of internal party democracy as a precondition for receiving public funds. Without all three, any new scheme will reproduce the opacity it claims to address.

## II. Election Commission Independence: The Appointment Question

The independence of the Election Commission of India — the constitutional body under Article 324 charged with superintendence, direction, and control of elections — has become a subject of renewed judicial and political contestation.

The Tarkunde Committee (1974) recommended that the Chief Election Commissioner be appointed on the advice of a committee comprising the Prime Minister, the Leader of Opposition in the Lok Sabha, and the Chief Justice of India. Five decades later, this recommendation remains unimplemented. The current appointment process — entirely at the discretion of the executive — creates a structural vulnerability that undermines institutional credibility regardless of the competence of individual appointees.

The Supreme Court's recent interventions — including its unprecedented step of involving the judiciary in the Special Intensive Revision (SIR) process in Bihar due to a perceived trust deficit between state governments and the Commission — signal that the judiciary itself views the current arrangement as insufficient.

### Constitutional Imperative

The Dinesh Goswami Committee recommended that the salary, service conditions, and removal protections of the Chief Election Commissioner be constitutionally secured on the analogy of Supreme Court judges. This remains the minimum threshold for credible reform. An Election Commission whose tenure, appointment, and removal are subject to executive discretion cannot be the impartial arbiter that Article 324 envisions.

## III. One Nation, One Election: Constitutional Ambition vs Federal Reality

The proposal for simultaneous elections to the Lok Sabha and all state legislative assemblies — endorsed by the Law Commission in 2025 — is perhaps the most structurally ambitious electoral reform under consideration. It requires at least 18 constitutional amendments, primarily to Article 83 (duration of Parliament) and Article 172 (duration of state assemblies).

### Arguments in Favour

Proponents cite the fiscal burden of perpetual elections (India is effectively in election mode every six months), the disruption caused by the Model Code of Conduct to governance and policy implementation, and the administrative strain on security forces and the Election Commission.

### The Federal Counterargument

The proposal, however, runs into a fundamental tension with India's federal design. Premature dissolution of state assemblies or artificial extension of their terms to align with a national cycle undermines the principle that state governments derive their mandate from state electorates on state-specific issues. As the Carnegie Endowment has noted, simultaneous elections could create perverse incentives — parties and lawmakers would have strong motivation to engage in backdoor deals to avoid government dissolution at all costs, introducing new forms of political horse-trading.

Moreover, the quasi-federal framing used to justify central override of state election cycles — that India's Constitution has a "clear Union bias" — conflates constitutional design with democratic practice. Federalism in India has evolved through convention, judicial interpretation, and political negotiation far beyond the text of 1950. Any reform that structurally subordinates state electoral autonomy must reckon with this evolution, not merely cite the original text.

## IV. Delimitation: The Population-Representation Dilemma

The constitutional freeze on delimitation — extended until after the next Census — has created a representation paradox. Southern states that successfully implemented population control policies now face a shrinking share of political power relative to northern states whose populations grew faster.

The proposals under consideration — expanding the Lok Sabha to 750–888 seats, or adopting a weighted formula (80 per cent population, 20 per cent governance indicators like literacy and health) — each carry trade-offs. Pure population-based delimitation penalises good governance. Weighted formulas introduce subjectivity. Seat expansion without redistricting merely inflates the legislature without resolving the underlying imbalance.

The deeper question is whether India's first-past-the-post system, designed for a nation of 360 million in 1950, remains fit for purpose in a democracy of 1.4 billion with radically different demographic trajectories across states. The Tarkunde Committee's early recommendation of proportional representation deserves renewed consideration — not as a replacement, but as a complementary mechanism for national representation.

---

## V. Digital Disruption: The Unregulated Frontier

India approaches the 2026 election cycle with over 900 million internet users and 65 per cent of citizens relying on social media for news. The electoral framework — designed for an era of print pamphlets and loudspeaker vans — has no meaningful regulatory architecture for AI-generated deepfakes, WhatsApp-driven misinformation cascades, or influencer-based campaign spending that falls outside expenditure reporting requirements.

The Election Commission's current powers to regulate digital campaigning are limited to voluntary codes of conduct negotiated with platforms — an arrangement that has proven inadequate globally. India needs a statutory framework for digital campaign transparency that includes mandatory labelling of AI-generated political content, real-time disclosure of digital advertising expenditure, and platform accountability for viral political misinformation during election periods.

---

## Conclusion: From Committee Reports to Constitutional Will

The history of electoral reform in India is, as scholars have noted, a history of much research and little implementation. From Tarkunde (1974) to the Law Commission (2025), the intellectual architecture for reform exists. What has been absent is the political will to implement recommendations that would constrain the very actors who must legislate them.

The Supreme Court's Electoral Bond verdict, the SIR process intervention, and the One Nation One Election debate have created a rare convergence of judicial activism, institutional introspection, and public attention. India's democratic renewal demands that this moment not be squandered on isolated fixes — a new funding scheme here, a delimitation formula there — but seized for the comprehensive reform that five decades of committees have outlined and no Parliament has delivered.

The question is no longer what reforms India needs. It is whether India's political class possesses the institutional imagination to reform the very system that sustains its power.
